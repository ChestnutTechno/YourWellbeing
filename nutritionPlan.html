Importance of Nutrition

How does nurition affect our health?

Our diet and mental health have a complicated relationship. However, there is a relationship between what we eat and how we feel, according to studies.

Our brain is affected by what we eat. Some meals can improve our mood. A Mediterranean-style diet that includes plenty of vegetables, seafood, fresh herbs, garlic, olive oil, cereals, and grains, as well as fish oil, can help to alleviate depressive symptoms.

What is Serotonin?

Seratonin is a neurotransmitter, or chemical messenger, that’s involved in many processes throughout your body, from regulating your mood to promoting smooth digestion.

It's also recognised for encouraging excellent sleep by assisting in the regulation of circadian rhythms, assisting in the regulation of hunger, contributing in the promotion of learning and memory, and helping in the promotion of pleasant moods and prosocial conduct.

Low Serotonin often make you feel:


  • Feeling jittery, down, or sad

  • Feeling angry or hostile

  • Sleep trouble or if you're tired

  • Feel irrational

  • A reduced appetite

  • Suffer from nausea and stomach problems



Strategies to boost serotonin levels

  1. Food

    You can't acquire serotonin directly from food, but you can receive tryptophan, an amino acid that your brain converts to serotonin. High-protein meals, such as turkey and salmon, are high in tryptophan. However, due to the blood-brain barrier, it's not as easy as consuming tryptophan-rich meals. This is a protective sheath that surrounds your brain and regulates what enters and exits it.

    In a nutshell, foods high in tryptophan are generally also high in other amino acids. These other amino acids are more likely than tryptophan to penetrate the blood-brain barrier because they are more plentiful.

    However, there may be a method to circumvent the mechanism. According to research, combining carbohydrates with meals high in tryptophan may enable more tryptophan to reach your brain. Consume 25 to 30 grammes of carbs with tryptophan-rich foods.


  2. Exercise

    Exercising causes tryptophan to be released into your bloodstream. It may also reduce the quantity of other amino acids in the body. More tryptophan will be able to enter your brain as a result of this.

    Aerobic activity at a level you're comfortable with appears to have the biggest impact, so dust off those roller skates or sign up for a dancing class. The objective is to raise your heart rate.


Nutrients that boost mental health

Many of us are well aware of the benefits that eating a balanced diet has on our physical health, but do you know how it benefits our mental health? More and more research is coming to light on the impact food has on both our overall mental wellness, as well as specific mental illnesses like depression and anxiety. Here we’ll take a closer look at how food and mood are related, what role our gut health has and how a nutrition professional can support you.

Select a nutrient and see how it affects your mood



Carbohydrate

In order for your brain to be able to concentrate and focus, it needs energy (20% of all energy needed by the body is used by the brain). This energy comes from blood glucose, and the glucose in our blood comes from the carbohydrates we eat.

When we don’t have enough energy for the brain, we can feel weak, tired and unable to think clearly. Ensuring you eat regular meals containing some carbohydrates will help with this. Alongside starchy foods like pasta and rice, sources of carbohydrates include:


Wholegrains


Fruits


Vegetables


Legumes



When your blood glucose rises and falls rapidly, it can have an impact on mood, making you feel irritable, low and even triggering symptoms of anxiety. Keeping your blood glucose levels steady throughout the day is key. Try foods that release energy slowly like oats, cereals, nuts and seeds, and aim to eat smaller portions spaced out throughout the day.

Proteins and fats

Alongside the energy it gets from carbohydrates, your brain needs amino acids to help regulate thoughts and feelings. As protein contains amino acids, it’s important to get enough of this in your diet. Protein is found in lean meat, fish, eggs, nuts and seeds, cheese, soya products and legumes.

Some people may be under the impression that all fat is bad for us, but this isn’t the case. Fatty acids, like omega-3 and omega-6, are essential for our brains to function well. Healthy fats can be found in nuts, seeds, oily fish, poultry, avocados, dairy products and eggs.

Our focus on low-fat diets may have also inadvertently affected our mental well-being. The brain is around 60% fat and omega 3 fatty acids are important for neurons to communicate effectively.

Vitamins

When we don’t get enough of certain vitamins and minerals, both our physical and mental health can suffer. The best way to ensure you’re getting enough of the vitamins and minerals you need is to eat a varied and balanced diet, rich in fruit and vegetables. For some, a supplement may be needed, but be sure to check this with your doctor or a nutrition professional.

Vitamin B: Not getting enough B1, B3 and B12 can make you feel low, tired and irritable. Animal protein foods such as meat, fish, eggs and dairy, and fortified cereals are rich in B vitamins.

Vitamin K and Folate: Vitamin K and folate, which help protect your brain against stroke. They also provide a boost to your memory and concentration. Avocados serve up a high dose of lutein, too, which studies have linked to improved brain function.

Minerals

When we don’t get enough of certain vitamins and minerals, both our physical and mental health can suffer. The best way to ensure you’re getting enough of the vitamins and minerals you need is to eat a varied and balanced diet, rich in fruit and vegetables. For some, a supplement may be needed, but be sure to check this with your doctor or a nutrition professional.

Iron: A lack of iron can lead you to feel weak, tired and lethargic. Foods rich in iron include red meat, poultry, fish, beans and pulses and fortified cereals.

Selenium: A selenium deficiency may increase the chance of feeling depressed and other negative mood states. Good sources of selenium include Brazil nuts, seeds, wholemeal bread, meat and fish.
